# PartsBase ICP Brief

*Read this before building any outbound workflow, writing any content, or building any lead scoring model.*

---

## What PartsBase Is

An aviation parts marketplace. Suppliers list their inventory. Buyers search for and source parts. PartsBase charges suppliers (sellers) for membership/access. It's a two-sided marketplace where supplier acquisition = revenue growth.

**The core value prop:**
- For buyers: Find any aviation part, fast, from verified suppliers globally
- For suppliers: Get in front of 7,600+ qualified aviation companies actively sourcing parts

---

## The Two Sides

### Side 1: Suppliers (Revenue-Generating — This Is Outbound's Target)

Who they are:
- Aviation parts distributors and stockists (the biggest segment)
- Surplus and aftermarket parts dealers
- OEM component manufacturers
- Parts brokers

What they care about:
- Leads — buyers actively looking for what they stock
- Reach — getting discovered by companies they can't cold-call
- Compliance — FAA/EASA traceability requirements (important: don't pitch them anything that sounds non-compliant)
- Speed — aviation AOG (aircraft on ground) situations are urgent, buyers pay premium

Outbound personas:
- **VP Sales / Sales Director** — cares about pipeline and close rates
- **Owner / General Manager** (at smaller distributors) — wears all hats, cost-sensitive
- **Marketing Manager** — may not exist at smaller shops

---

### Side 2: Buyers (Demand Side — Inbound, Nurture Focus)

Who they are:
- MRO shops (maintenance, repair, overhaul) — highest frequency buyers
- Commercial airline procurement and fleet maintenance teams (Delta, Air France, etc.)
- Charter and regional operators
- Defense contractors (Northrop Grumman, Lockheed, etc.)
- Corporate aviation operators

What they care about:
- Part availability and traceability documentation (8130, CoC)
- Price competitiveness
- Supplier reliability and reputation
- Speed of fulfillment (AOG situations especially)

---

## Aviation Domain Knowledge (Important for Outreach)

Don't write outbound copy without understanding these terms. Using them correctly signals credibility; getting them wrong kills deals.

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| AOG | Aircraft on Ground — urgent situation, plane can't fly, need parts NOW |
| MRO | Maintenance, Repair & Overhaul — shops that service aircraft |
| OEM | Original Equipment Manufacturer — Boeing, Airbus, Honeywell, etc. |
| 8130 | FAA airworthiness approval tag — required for most US aviation parts |
| CoC | Certificate of Conformance — supplier quality documentation |
| ATA codes | Standardized system for categorizing aircraft components by chapter |
| Traceability | Documentation chain proving a part's origin and handling history |
| Surplus | Excess inventory parts, often at discount, require extra traceability |

## Outbound Targeting Signals (for Clay enrichment)

High-intent signals to look for when building Clay enrichment tables:

**For supplier targeting:**
- Company type: aviation parts distributor, stockist, surplus dealer
- LinkedIn keywords: "aviation parts," "aerospace components," "aircraft spares"
- Company size: 10-200 employees (sweet spot — large enough to have inventory, small enough to need marketplace reach)
- Missing from LinkedIn: no mention of PartsBase or similar platforms
- Geography: US (primary), EU, UK, Middle East (secondary)

**Negative signals (skip):**
- Airlines themselves — they're buyers, not suppliers
- MRO shops — buyers, not suppliers (unless they also sell surplus)
- Defense primes (Northrop, Lockheed) — already have enterprise contracts

## Tone for Outbound

Aviation is a relationship-driven, trust-first industry. Compliance and safety are non-negotiable concerns.

**Do:**
- Lead with credibility (7,600 companies, Delta, Northrop, Air France as proof)
- Speak the language (AOG, MRO, traceability, ATA)
- Be specific about their segment and what they sell
- Reference urgency without overselling

**Don't:**
- Sound like a generic SaaS tool pitch
- Promise anything compliance-related you can't back up
- Use marketing buzzwords that don't land in B2B aviation
- Mass-blast without personalization — aviation is small, word gets around
